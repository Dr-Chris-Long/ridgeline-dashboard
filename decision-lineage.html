DECISION LINEAGE TOOL

Theoretical design for NEPA decision traceability and deliberative classification
Proposed pilot under OMB M-25-21 and DOI Secretarial Order 3444
THEORETICAL DESIGN — NOT YET BUILT — REQUIRES AUTHORIZED INFRASTRUCTURE

What it is

The Decision Lineage Tool is a proposed AI-assisted capability to trace how each substantive decision in a final NEPA record was shaped by its inputs. Given a completed project file, the tool identifies every decision node in the final document, traces that node backward through drafts and inputs to determine what actually influenced it, and proposes a deliberative-vs-final classification for each contributing element with supporting reasoning.

This extends RIDGELINE's existing compliance review capability into the administrative record itself. RIDGELINE today evaluates an EA against legal obligations. The Decision Lineage Tool answers a different question: how did this EA arrive at the language it arrived at, and which pieces of the record are deliberative under FOIA Exemption 5.

Primary functions

  1. Decision node identification. Every substantive language change from earliest draft to signed final is identified and isolated as a decision node.
  2. Influence mapping. For each decision node, the tool traces backward through the record to identify which inputs (comments, field data, BiOp terms, IDT discussion, Solicitor review, draft redlines) most likely caused the change.
  3. Deliberative classification. Each decision node and its contributing inputs are proposed as deliberative or final, with reasoning, citations, and a confidence score. All classifications are proposed. Human FOIA staff make final determinations.
  4. Causal map output. The complete lineage for the document is output as a queryable causal map with FOIA-ready exemption recommendations, decision-to-input traceability, and administrative record completeness flags.

Alignment with stated pilot proposal

This tool is the full-build articulation of the pilot proposed to DOI on March 16, 2026. The original proposal scoped a Power Automate workflow integrated with DOI's AI Hub to process large PDF documents, extract text, and analyze content to identify key decision points. The design below expands the methodology while holding the original scope boundaries: historical completed projects, public records for validation, zero additional budget, 30-day pilot.

Why this is theory, not product. The real operational value of this tool requires access to deliberative documents — IDT notes, draft redlines, Solicitor correspondence, pre-decisional PST archives. None of that can be processed outside authorized DOI infrastructure. The design below is complete. The pilot is proposed against public historical records to validate methodology before any deliberative data is touched.

The problem

BLM currently has no systematic method for reconstructing how a final NEPA decision was shaped. When a litigant, a court, or a FOIA requester asks why does the final EA say this instead of what the draft said, the answer depends on whoever was in the room at the time and whatever notes they took. Institutional knowledge walks out the door with staff turnover. FOIA Exemption 5 determinations are made document by document under time pressure. Litigation exposure on deliberative-process privilege claims is highest precisely where records are thinnest.

What this changes

FOIA response time

Exemption 5 classifications are proposed per document section with reasoning, instead of relying on staff to reconstruct deliberative status from scratch.

Litigation defensibility

When a plaintiff challenges a decision, the agency can demonstrate the exact inputs that shaped the final language — not from memory, but from the record.

Administrative record completeness

Gaps between what the final language cites and what is actually in the AR are flagged. No more discovering a missing document at the PI hearing.

Institutional memory

Decisions are traceable across staff turnover. A planner arriving three years after a project can see what drove each paragraph without asking anyone.

What this is not. This tool does not make FOIA determinations. It does not classify documents automatically. It does not replace legal review. Every classification is proposed and requires human confirmation. The tool produces structured recommendations with reasoning — FOIA and NEPA staff review, override where appropriate, and make the determinations that become final.

Four-layer architecture

The tool processes a project file through four sequential layers. Each layer produces structured outputs consumed by the next. All processing is reproducible, auditable, and re-runnable.

Layer 1: Corpus ingestion

Every document associated with the project is ingested: EA drafts in sequence, final signed EA, FONSI, Decision Record, public comments and responses, BiOp and ESA consultation documents, IDT meeting notes, field survey data, Solicitor correspondence, email chains where reviewable. Each document is parsed to page-level and section-level granularity with timestamp metadata. An initial deliberative flag is applied based on document type (drafts = deliberative; final signed documents = final; other categories = unclear pending analysis). This is the same ingestion pattern RIDGELINE already uses for EA text extraction, extended to cover the full project file.

Layer 2: Language diff engine

The system compares the earliest draft of each EA section against the signed final language and identifies every substantive change. Cosmetic edits (typos, formatting, citation style) are filtered out. What remains is the set of decision nodes — the places where the document actually moved. Each decision node captures the before-state, the after-state, the section, and the timestamp of the version where the change first appeared.

Layer 3: Influence mapping

For each decision node, the tool examines all inputs present in the record before the version where the change first appeared. It scores each input on three dimensions: semantic similarity (does the input's language or concept appear in the final change), temporal proximity (how close in time was this input to the change), and co-occurrence (do the same terms, authorities, or framing conventions appear in both). Scores are combined into a confidence value that the input materially influenced the decision node. Inputs are ranked from highest to lowest probable influence. Multi-input nodes are supported — most real decisions are shaped by more than one thing.

Layer 4: Classification output

Each decision node and its ranked contributing inputs are proposed for classification. The output per node is: the decision node itself (deliberative or final), the contributing inputs (deliberative, final, or unclear), proposed FOIA treatment for each (Exemption 5 deliberative process privilege or releasable), confidence score, and reasoning that cites the relevant authorities (FOIA statute, DOJ FOIA Guide, agency precedent). All outputs are proposals. All require human confirmation. Low-confidence classifications are automatically flagged for legal review rather than being posted.

Same cost envelope

Compound value

Each project processed through RIDGELINE strengthens the lineage tool's reference base for the next project. Institutional learning accrues to the system, not to individual memory.

The integration story in one sentence. RIDGELINE already knows what a project says and whether it complies with the law. The Decision Lineage Tool adds the ability to know how the project arrived at what it says, and which parts of that arrival are deliberative under FOIA.

The full operational value of the Decision Lineage Tool requires processing deliberative documents. These are exactly the documents that cannot be touched outside an authorized DOI environment with appropriate FOIA-exemption-aware handling.

What the 30-day pilot would do instead

The pilot validates the methodology against historical public records only. No deliberative documents are processed. No CUI is handled. The goal of the pilot is not to produce real FOIA classifications — it is to demonstrate that the four-layer methodology produces consistent, defensible, reproducible outputs on known test cases.

  1. 3-5 historical Medford projects that have already completed their FOIA cycle. We know the answers. The tool's proposed classifications can be compared against the actual final Exemption 5 determinations made by humans.
  2. Public-only corpus. EAs, final decisions, public comments, BLM responses, published scientific references. No internal documents. No deliberative material.
  3. Baseline metrics. Time per project for manual FOIA review vs. tool-assisted review. Classification agreement rate between tool proposal and human determination. Error type inventory — where does the tool get it wrong and why.
  4. Replicable workflow artifact. End of pilot produces a documented, reviewable procedure that other districts could adopt if DOI chooses to scale.
Hard boundary. The pilot explicitly excludes any document that has not already been released publicly or completed its FOIA cycle with a final determination. Validation data only. No production classifications.

Decision point at pilot end

If the pilot validates methodology on public records, the next step is a proposal to DOI OFCIO and the Solicitor's Office for authorized deliberative access under appropriate handling controls. If the pilot does not validate, the methodology is revised or the tool is abandoned. Either outcome produces value: a working capability or a documented negative result that saves DOI from investing further in an unworkable approach.

When the tool is operational, each project processed produces the following structured outputs. The pilot produces the same output schema on public records for methodology validation.

Every substantive language change from draft to final, captured with before/after text, section, document, and timestamp.
Per-node input map
Ranked list of contributing inputs per decision node with confidence scores (0.0 – 1.0) and reasoning.
Proposed classification table
Deliberative / final / unclear for each decision node and contributing input, with proposed FOIA Exemption 5 treatment and supporting authority citations.
Administrative record completeness report
Decision nodes where contributing inputs cited in the final record are not present in the ingested corpus — potential AR gaps surfaced before certification.
Legal review queue
All low-confidence classifications automatically routed to FOIA Officer and Solicitor for human determination, with the tool's reasoning and confidence delta.
Project lineage map (visual)
Queryable causal graph showing how the final EA's language was assembled from its inputs. Navigable by section, by input type, by time.
FOIA response package
Exemption 5 determination proposals per section with reasoning, formatted for FOIA staff review and adoption or override.
Audit log
Per-run record of every model call, every input, every classification proposed. SO 3444 compliant. Reproducible by re-running against the same corpus.

What a FOIA staff member actually does with this

  1. Opens the project. Sees the list of decision nodes and their proposed classifications.
  2. Reviews the high-confidence "final" proposals (released) and the high-confidence "deliberative" proposals (withheld). Confirms or overrides each.
  3. Works through the flagged "unclear" queue with the Solicitor's Office. Low volume because most close calls are concentrated in specific document types the tool surfaces explicitly.
  4. Exports the final FOIA response with per-section exemption reasoning already drafted. Edits and approves.
  5. Finalizes the release. The same classifications become the durable record for the next FOIA request on the same project.
The operational saving. Manual FOIA review on a 400-page NEPA record typically runs 40–80 hours. Tool-assisted review targets 8–15 hours with equal or better Exemption 5 defensibility, with the bulk of remaining staff time focused on the close calls that genuinely require human judgment.

Control boundaries — what the tool cannot do

  • Cannot release any document to any external party. No document output leaves the authorized environment without human approval.
  • Cannot make a final Exemption 5 determination. All determinations are made by the FOIA Officer.
  • Cannot classify a document as non-deliberative without a supporting input map. Low-confidence classifications are automatically escalated.
  • Cannot process documents without the deliberative flag being human-set on ingestion. Deliberative status of a source document is not a tool output.
  • Cannot train on deliberative material. The tool's reasoning models are not fine-tuned on agency data.
  • Cannot operate on active projects during the pilot. Historical completed projects only.

Audit and reproducibility

Every run produces a complete audit record: the corpus ingested with hashes, the model calls made with inputs and outputs, the classifications proposed with reasoning, the human decisions made on each proposal, and the final outputs released. Runs are reproducible — given the same corpus and the same model versions, re-running the tool produces the same classifications. Any divergence is itself an auditable event.

Standing caveat. This tool is pre-decisional and internal. Its output is not a legal opinion. Classifications are proposed, not final. All FOIA determinations remain the responsibility of the FOIA Officer in consultation with the Solicitor's Office.
